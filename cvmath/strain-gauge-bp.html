Strain Gauge Pressure Transducer

Physics, Mathematics & Annotated Schematics
Operating Principle
A thin silicon or stainless diaphragm separates the blood (via heparinised saline column) from the transducer. Pressure deflects the diaphragm, straining bonded resistive elements that alter their resistance according to the piezoresistive effect.
Piezoresistive Effect
Applied mechanical stress changes the resistivity of semiconductor (silicon) or metallic alloy gauges. Unlike simple geometric deformation, silicon exhibits a large piezoresistive coefficient, giving gauge factors of 50–150 (vs ~2 for metal foil).
Clinical Context
In the Cath lab, an arterial line or pressure wire (e.g. RadiWire for FFR) transmits pressure via fluid column or direct piezoelectric contact to the transducer, which is zeroed to atmospheric pressure at the phlebostatic axis (right atrium level).
Output & Calibration
Standard output is 5 μV/V/mmHg. With 5 V excitation, a 200 mmHg pressure yields ~5 mV full-scale. The amplifier chain delivers 0–10 V or 4–20 mA to the haemodynamic recording system.

Wheatstone Bridge
Half-bridge configuration: R₁ and R₂ are active gauges bonded to opposite faces of the diaphragm. Tensile strain increases R₁; compressive strain decreases R₂ by the same ΔR. R₃ and R₄ are matched reference resistors on the unstressed housing, providing temperature compensation.
