Non-Invasive Blood Pressure
Oscillometric Transduction

Physics · Signal Processing · Mathematical Foundations

I. Physical Principle

A pneumatic cuff is inflated around the upper arm above systolic pressure, occluding the brachial artery. As the cuff deflates, arterial pressure pulses transmit mechanical oscillations into the cuff air column. A piezoelectric or capacitive pressure transducer converts these pressure fluctuations into a voltage signal — the oscillometric envelope. Systolic (SBP), mean arterial pressure (MAP), and diastolic (DBP) are inferred from the envelope's shape.

II. Arterial States During Deflation

The cuff transmits external pressure Pext circumferentially. The artery wall acts as a pressure-transmitting membrane — only when Part ≈ Pext does wall tension vanish and the vessel become maximally compliant, yielding peak oscillation amplitude.

III. Wall Mechanics — Laplace & Compliance

Law of Laplace (cylindrical vessel)
T = ΔP · r / h T = circumferential wall tension [N/m] ΔP = transmural pressure = P_art − P_cuff r = arterial radius h = wall thickness

When Pcuff → Part, transmural pressure ΔP → 0, so T → 0. The artery is at zero-tension: a tiny pressure change causes a large volume change — maximum compliance.

Arterial Compliance
C(P) = dV/dP C is maximal at ΔP = 0 → peak oscillations C falls for ΔP >> 0 or ΔP << 0
Pressure Transmission into Cuff
ΔP_cuff = (A_art / A_cuff) · C(P) · dP_art/dt A_art = artery cross-sectional area A_cuff = cuff bladder area dP_art/dt = rate of pressure change per beat

The transducer (piezoelectric or MEMS capacitive) converts ΔPcuff to voltage via the piezoelectric equation: V = g · σ · t, where g is the piezoelectric voltage constant, σ stress, t thickness.

IV. Oscillometric Envelope & Signal Morphology

Cuff pressure (slow ramp) Oscillation amplitude envelope Individual oscillations
Systolic (SBP)
A(P_cuff = SBP) = k_SBP · A_max k_SBP ≈ 0.45–0.55

SBP identified when rising envelope reaches ~50% of peak amplitude.

Mean Arterial Pressure
MAP = P_cuff at A_max (peak oscillation)

MAP is directly read as the cuff pressure at maximum oscillation — the most robust measurement.

Diastolic (DBP)
A(P_cuff = DBP) = k_DBP · A_max k_DBP ≈ 0.70–0.85

DBP identified when falling envelope drops to ~70–85% of peak. k values are device-specific, validated against auscultation.

V. Transducer Signal Chain

Piezo: V = g₃₃ · σ · t MEMS: C = ε₀A / d(P) V_out = V_bias · ΔC/C₀ Envelope: A(n) = max|x(k)| for k ∈ [n-W, n+W] (window W)

VI. MAP Estimation & Error Sources

Mean Arterial Pressure (exact)
MAP = DBP + ¹⁄₃(SBP − DBP) = DBP + ¹⁄₃ · PP
Oscillometric MAP (direct)
MAP_osc = P_cuff(A_max) Most accurate NIBP reading. Error typically < 5 mmHg vs invasive.
Pulse Pressure
PP = SBP − DBP Normal: 40 mmHg Wide PP → aortic regurgitation Narrow PP → cardiac tamponade

Error sources:

  • Cuff sizing (under/over-sized — Laplace geometry error)
  • Arrhythmia — irregular envelope; algorithm fails
  • Motion artefact (≥ 2 Hz overlap with cardiac signal)
  • Arterial stiffness — shifts compliance curve, skews k-ratios
  • Improper arm position (hydrostatic offset: ≈ 0.77 mmHg/cm)

VII. Hydrostatic Pressure Correction

Blood pressure varies with the vertical height of the measurement site relative to the heart — a direct consequence of fluid statics. Any NIBP measurement must be corrected if the cuff is not at heart level.

P_corrected = P_measured + ρ · g · Δh ρ = blood density ≈ 1060 kg/m³ g = 9.81 m/s² Δh = height of cuff below (+) or above (−) heart level Numerically: ΔP ≈ 0.77 mmHg per cm of height difference Example: cuff 5 cm above heart → P_corrected = P_measured − 3.85 mmHg

Clinical guideline: cuff should be at the level of the right atrium (midsternal, ~4th intercostal space). For wrist devices, raise the wrist to heart level before measurement.
